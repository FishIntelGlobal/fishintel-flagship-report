Executive Summary

Incident type: Capsize during ship assist towage. A conventional tug, operating as the stern tug for a passenger vessel navigating a Scottish port channel, girted, capsized, and sank. Both crew members were lost. Classified as a Very Serious Marine Casualty.

Core question: Did environmental conditions contribute to the capsize, and was the operational decision window constrained by weather at any point during the towage?

Method: ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis was extracted across a five-point window (12:00–16:00 UTC) at the incident coordinates and processed through the FishIntel deterministic reconstruction pipeline. This is an atmospheric-only reconstruction; ERA5 wave data is unavailable at this enclosed estuarine location.

Key result: Environmental conditions were operationally benign throughout. At the decision frame (7.3 kt sustained, improving to 6.1 kt by 16:00), the reconstruction classifies risk as LOW and the Tirr framework returns REGIME 1 — REGIME_1_SAFE. The full environmental decision budget was intact at the moment of capsize. The loss was not environmentally forced.

On 24 February 2023, the passenger vessel was returning to port after earlier trials, with a pilot embarked. The vessel entered the navigational channel at approximately 15:15. The tug made fast aft as the stern tug. Conditions were unremarkable: the MAIB report records NW Beaufort force 2, good visibility, sea state 2, and high water, slack tidal stream.

The tug was unable to reverse direction to operate directly astern before its weight came onto the towing bridle. The gob rope did not prevent the tug from being towed sideways. The passenger vessel's speed produced towline loads estimated at between two and five times the port's recommended speed range for conventional tug operations. The tug girted, capsized, and sank. Both crew members were lost.

The MAIB investigation identified fourteen safety issues directly contributing to the casualty, and a further six indirectly contributing. The principal factors relevant to this environmental reconstruction are summarised in Section 05 as condensed neutral data statements.

Key Analytical Finding

Primary Finding

The environmental conditions were operationally benign. The decision budget was fully intact. The capsize was not environmentally forced.

At the decision frame (15:00 UTC), ERA5 records 7.3 kt sustained wind, 17.1 kt gusts, good visibility, and stable barometric pressure. Wind speed was declining throughout the extraction window — 7.6 kt at 12:00 falling to 6.1 kt at 16:00. The Tirr framework classifies the situation as Regime 1 — REGIME_1_SAFE, with the effective Tirr at the regime ceiling. No environmental escalation of any kind was detected.

The capsize resulted from compound operational factors: the tug was unable to reverse direction to operate directly astern before its weight came onto the towing bridle, the gob rope did not prevent it from being towed sideways, the passenger vessel's speed produced towline loads estimated at two to five times the port's recommended range for conventional tug operations, and there was incomplete information exchange between the pilot and the tug. No environmental forcing capable of contributing to the capsize was detected.

This reconstruction quantifies what the environment did not do. It did not deteriorate. It did not exceed any operational threshold. It did not reduce the time available for intervention. The entire margin between safe operation and catastrophe was consumed by the operational configuration — not by weather.

This report does not attribute fault or negligence to any individual. Its purpose is to establish, with quantified precision, the environmental context in which the operational decisions were made.

Methodology

FishIntel Global reconstructs environmental conditions using ECMWF ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis — the same dataset relied upon by meteorological agencies, climate researchers, and aviation safety authorities worldwide. ERA5 provides hourly estimates on a global grid at approximately 31 km horizontal resolution.

All values pass through a unit-gated validation layer enforcing explicit conversion factors and plausibility bounds using Decimal arithmetic. The pipeline is deterministic: identical inputs always produce identical outputs.

This reconstruction is atmospheric-only. ERA5 wave data from the WAM ocean wave model is unavailable at the incident location because the upper Firth of Clyde is classified as land in the ERA5 wave grid at 0.5° resolution. The MAIB observation of sea state 2 (estimated 0.2 m) is cited as contextual information but does not enter the Tier 1 evidence chain.

Closing Statement

Not every maritime casualty is driven by weather. In sheltered waters, on a calm day, the environmental reconstruction may confirm what experienced mariners already suspect — that the conditions were benign, and that the loss must be explained by something other than the environment.

In this case, ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis confirms exactly that. Wind speeds were light and declining. Pressure was stable. Visibility was good. The Tirr framework classifies the environmental situation as Regime 1 — Safe, with the full decision budget intact at the moment of capsize.

The casualty was driven by the interaction of towing geometry, vessel speed, information exchange, and the physical limitations of the gob rope arrangement. The environment was not the driver of the casualty. The question this reconstruction leaves for investigators, insurers, and regulators is not whether the weather was dangerous — it was not — but how to assess operational risk when the hazard is embedded entirely in the operational configuration itself.
