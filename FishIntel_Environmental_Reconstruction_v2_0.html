01

Executive Summary

Incident type: Capsize and foundering. A ~14 m UK-registered steel stern trawler, operating single-handed in the English Channel, capsized and sank during recovery of a heavy trawl catch. The skipper survived after boarding the vessel’s liferaft. The vessel was a total constructive loss. No fatalities.

Core question: What environmental conditions existed at the time of the capsize, and what was their relationship to the vessel’s stability envelope?

Method: ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data was extracted at the incident coordinates across a four-point time window (06:00–11:00 UTC) and processed through the FishIntel deterministic reconstruction pipeline. The reconstruction quantifies sustained wind, gust speed, significant wave height, atmospheric pressure, temperature, and visibility at each extraction point.

Key result: ERA5 reconstruction shows sustained 10 m winds of 15.3–16.7 knots (Beaufort Force 4), gusts of 22.9–23.7 knots, and significant wave height of 0.82–0.88 metres across the incident window. Wind direction was consistently northerly (006°–010°). Visibility was good. Atmospheric pressure was near-normal (1013.4–1014.1 hPa). The FishIntel pipeline classifies the environmental risk as LOW to MODERATE (36.7–41.1%). For a vessel with adequate stability reserves, these conditions would have been routine.

Analytical finding: This was not a case of extreme weather overwhelming a vessel. The environmental conditions were moderate, stable, and foreseeable — they matched the published forecast. The evidence indicates that the capsize occurred when the vessel’s stability reserves were critically thin. Stability analysis conducted during the subsequent investigation placed the vessel only 35 mm above the danger-of-capsize zone, where the maximum recommended sea state is 0.8 m. ERA5 shows significant wave height of 0.82–0.88 m, which was below the amber-zone maximum of 1.6 m but close to, and marginally above, the 0.8 m threshold associated with the red danger-of-capsize zone. Routine environmental conditions became unsafe because vessel-specific safety margins were already critically reduced through accumulated modifications, low fuel, seized freeing ports, and operational loading from a high gantry point. The information required to calculate this vulnerability existed before the vessel sailed, but no such calculation had been undertaken at the time of the incident.

The vessel departed its home port at approximately 0640 local time, heading to established fishing grounds in the English Channel to trawl for demersal fish species. The skipper was operating single-handed for the second consecutive day due to crewing difficulties. The vessel had been unable to take fuel before departure as the harbour fuel pump was not working.

The vessel arrived at the fishing grounds approximately one hour later and deployed a single demersal trawl net. After approximately two hours of trawling, the skipper began hauling the first catch. The powered net drum struggled under an excessive load. The skipper varied course and speed to wash accumulated mud and sand from the net. The first Gilson rope parted under load — an early indicator of excessive weight in the cod end.

After rigging a replacement Gilson rope over the starboard side of the aft gantry, the skipper recovered a partial load of approximately 1.5 tonnes of mud, sand, gravel, starfish and some fish onto the main deck. At least another 1.5 tonnes remained in the net. When the cod end cleared the sea surface during a final recovery attempt, it slewed to starboard. The vessel heeled, the deck edge became immersed, water poured on deck, the catch already on deck slid to starboard, and the vessel capsized rapidly to starboard and sank.

The skipper, wearing a PFD with a personal locator beacon, entered the water, swam to the vessel’s liferaft, and raised the alarm via mobile phone. The skipper was recovered uninjured approximately 40 minutes later by a nearby vessel.

Analytical Note — Wave Height Divergence

The investigation report describes seas of 1.5 m in Force 4 conditions. ERA5 reconstruction shows a significant wave height of 0.85 m at the incident coordinates. This divergence of approximately 0.65 m requires transparent treatment.

ERA5 is a gridded reanalysis product at approximately 31 km horizontal resolution. It does not capture localised effects including: shallow-water wave steepening over the variable-depth seabed; tidal current interaction with northerly waves; or the visual sea-state impression experienced at deck level on a ~14 m vessel, which includes whitecapping, spray, and vessel motion that compounds the perceived severity beyond the statistical significant wave height.

This report presents both values. The ERA5 value of 0.85 m represents the gridded significant wave height. The narrative value of 1.5 m represents the local description from the investigation. The vessel stability comparison considers both as the lower and upper bounds of the actual sea state.

Decision Window / Tirr Assessment

FishIntel’s Time-to-Irreversibility (Tirr) framework identifies when an operational situation becomes irreversible — the point beyond which no reasonable intervention by the vessel’s crew can change the outcome. In this case, the Tirr analysis addresses a fundamentally different mechanism from the first flagship reconstruction: the decision window closed not through environmental escalation, but through progressive consumption of stability reserves.

Tirr in this case is driven by vessel dynamics, not environmental progression. The environmental state remained within the same regime throughout. Irreversibility was reached when the suspended catch created a capsizing moment that exceeded the vessel’s remaining righting capacity.

At departure, the situation was in Regime 1: the decision window was fully open. The environment was moderate and foreseeable. When the excessive load became apparent and the first Gilson rope parted, the situation moved into Regime 2: corrective action was still available (the catch could have been released), but the margin was narrowing with each additional tonne on deck. When the cod end cleared the water and slewed to starboard, the situation entered Regime 3 within seconds: the capsizing moment exceeded the remaining righting capacity and no intervention could arrest the roll.

The environmental conditions played no escalating role in this progression. The Tirr transition from Regime 1 to Regime 3 appears to have been determined by the operational loading sequence acting against a vessel with critically depleted stability reserves.

Methodology

FishIntel Global reconstructs the environmental conditions surrounding maritime incidents using ECMWF ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data — the same dataset relied upon by meteorological agencies, climate researchers, and aviation safety authorities worldwide.

ERA5 provides hourly estimates of atmospheric, land-surface, and ocean-wave variables on a global grid at approximately 31 km horizontal resolution. It assimilates millions of observations per cycle and is continuously quality-controlled by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts. ERA5 data is freely available and independently verifiable by any party.

The FishIntel reconstruction pipeline extracts environmental variables at the precise coordinates and time window of each incident. All values pass through a unit-gated validation layer that enforces explicit conversion factors and plausibility bounds using Decimal arithmetic — preventing the silent unit errors that are a known source of analytical failure in environmental data processing. The pipeline is deterministic: identical inputs always produce identical outputs.

This reconstruction operates within defined boundaries. ERA5 is a gridded reanalysis product, not a point measurement. It does not capture localised effects smaller than its grid resolution, such as harbour-scale wind funnelling or shallow-water wave refraction. Where a case involves such effects, the reconstruction states this limitation explicitly. The system is designed to produce defensible evidence, not to overstate its precision.

FishIntel’s role is complementary to national maritime accident investigation. Investigation bodies establish causes and circumstances. FishIntel quantifies the environmental context in which operational decisions were made, using data sources that existed at the time of those decisions. The focus is on what was knowable, not on who is to blame.

Closing Statement

Maritime incidents are frequently described after the fact using qualitative or semi-quantitative environmental descriptions: “strong winds,” “heavy swell,” “poor visibility.” These descriptions are valuable for narrative understanding but do not quantify the decision-time environment with the precision required by insurers, regulators, and legal professionals.

This reconstruction addresses a different and equally important category of incident: one where the environmental conditions were not extreme. Force 4 winds. Sub-metre wave height. Good visibility. The kind of conditions in which experienced fishermen go to work. Environmental reconstruction confirms this ordinariness — and reveals why it matters.

The vessel that capsized on this day had a stability margin so thin that conditions which appeared routine were, for this specific vessel in this specific configuration, at the boundary of the danger zone. The Wolfson Guidance — a calculation requiring only two measurements, both known — would have shown this. The Met Office forecast was accurate. The environmental conditions were foreseeable. What was missing was the bridge between environmental knowledge and vessel-specific vulnerability.

This is the gap that foreseeability intelligence is designed to close. Not by predicting accidents, but by quantifying the conditions under which they become possible — before the critical moment arrives.
