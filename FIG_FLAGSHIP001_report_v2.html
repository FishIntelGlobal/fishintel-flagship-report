Executive Summary

Incident type: Grounding following anchor drag. A foreign-registered bulk carrier, anchored in ballast at a UK coastal anchorage, dragged its anchor during a severe winter storm and grounded on a public beach. Successfully refloated. No injuries, no pollution.

Core question: What environmental conditions were objectively knowable at the time the decision was made to remain at anchor on a lee shore overnight, and at what point did those conditions make the vessel's position untenable?

Method: ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data was extracted at the anchorage coordinates across a five-point time window (21:00–05:00 UTC) and processed through the FishIntel deterministic reconstruction pipeline.

Key result: The vessel was in an ELEVATED-risk environmental regime from 21:00 UTC, escalating to EXTREME risk by 00:00 UTC — five hours before the grounding. All three monitored environmental signals (wind, gust, wave) exceeded safety thresholds simultaneously at 21:00 UTC, providing an 8-hour intervention window.

Analytical finding: Environmental reconstruction shows conditions were deteriorating gradually and predictably across the entire incident window. The narrative description of Force 9–10 appears to conflate locally generated wind with remotely generated swell. ERA5 shows sustained wind of 20–27 knots (Beaufort force 5–7), not the 47–55 knots implied by the narrative Beaufort classification.

The vessel had sailed from a European port following a lengthy port state control detention. Unable to take bunkers before departure due to unpaid debts, she was ordered to proceed to North Africa for refuelling but had insufficient fuel for the passage.

She anchored in the bay approximately 1 nautical mile off a public beach, in a charted depth of 17 metres, using the port anchor with 7 shackles of cable.

On the evening before the grounding, the master was aware that the weather forecast predicted southerly winds up to Beaufort force 8. He wrote in his night orders that the main engine should be at immediate readiness for wind speeds exceeding 16–17 m/s.

By 0400 the following morning, the wind was gusting over 52 knots with a 4–5 metre swell. At 0425, the anchor began to drag. Despite full engine power and helm, the master was unable to turn the vessel into the wind. She grounded at 0525.

The vessel was refloated by harbour tugs at 1415 the same day. An underwater survey revealed hull plate deformation, breached tanks, and partial bilge keel detachment. There were no injuries and no pollution.

Analytical Note

The narrative account describes conditions as Beaufort force 9 to 10 (47–55 knots sustained). ERA5 reconstruction shows 10 m sustained wind of 27.0 knots (Beaufort force 6–7) at the anchorage position. These are not equivalent measurements and the difference is analytically significant.

ERA5 reports the 10-metre neutral-stability wind speed averaged over a ~31 km grid cell. The MAIB Beaufort classification is derived from sea-state observation at the vessel’s position — a method that conflates locally generated wind waves with remotely generated swell.

The dominant forcing mechanisms for anchor drag in this case were heavy swell loading on a vessel in ballast combined with severe gust loading (45 kt at 10 m, likely exceeding 50 kt at bridge height). Sustained local Force 9–10 wind was not required to produce the observed outcome.

T_irr Interpretation

T_irr estimates the physical time before the vessel’s situation becomes irreversible. T_practical estimates the minimum time a competent crew would need to recognise the hazard, assess the situation, and begin effective intervention. The margin is the difference: positive margin means intervention was physically possible.

Foreseeability Test

This report assesses foreseeability by applying the following test:

  1. The environmental data required to assess the risk existed and was accessible at the time of the decision (ERA5 reanalysis confirms this retrospectively; forecast data confirms it prospectively).
  2. The data was sufficient to identify that conditions at the anchorage position would exceed the operational envelope for a vessel of this type, size, and ballast condition.
  3. A reasonable operator with access to this data would have identified the risk of anchor drag under the forecast conditions and considered proceeding to sea or relocating to a more sheltered anchorage.
  4. The environmental progression from forecast conditions to actual conditions was continuous, not discontinuous — there was no sudden, unforeseeable step-change that made the outcome unavoidable.

This report does not determine causes or assign responsibility. The foreseeability test evaluates environmental evidence only.

Key Analytical Finding

Key Finding

Environmental reconstruction separates local wind forcing from remotely generated swell, revealing that the conventional Beaufort force 9–10 classification in the narrative account conflated two distinct physical mechanisms. ERA5 reanalysis shows 10 m sustained winds of 20.1–27.0 knots (Beaufort force 5–7) at the anchorage position, with gusts of 32.0–44.8 knots and significant wave height of 2.7–4.1 metres. All three environmental signals exceeded safety thresholds at 21:00 UTC, providing an 8-hour intervention window before the grounding.

Pipeline Classification

CRITICAL: Environmental reconstruction shows the vessel entered an ELEVATED risk regime at 21:00 UTC and an EXTREME risk regime by 00:00 UTC — five full hours before the grounding. The dominant forcing was heavy swell (3.8–4.1 m SWH) propagating into the anchorage from the open English Channel, combined with severe gust loading (41.6–44.8 kt). Sustained 10 m wind of 20–27 kt (Beaufort force 5–7) was materially lower than the narrative Force 9–10 description.

This finding is derived from the quantified environmental reconstruction, not from the narrative account. It represents what was objectively knowable at the time the critical operational decision was made, based on environmental data that existed and was accessible.

This report is an environmental reconstruction. It does not make determinations about individual conduct, operational adequacy, or regulatory compliance. Its purpose is to quantify the environmental conditions under which operational decisions were made.

Analytical Boundaries

The following boundaries apply to this reconstruction. Where a boundary introduces directional bias, the direction is stated explicitly so that the reader can assess the weight to place on each finding.

  • ERA5 reconstructs conditions on a ~31 km grid. It does not resolve harbour-scale wind acceleration, cliff-edge effects, or shallow-water wave transformation at the anchorage. Where these effects are present, actual conditions at the vessel's position are typically more severe than the gridded values. The environmental values in this report should therefore be interpreted as conservative lower bounds for the conditions experienced at the anchorage.
  • ERA5 gust speed represents the maximum instantaneous wind at the 10-metre reference height. On a 180-metre vessel in ballast with high windage, the wind speed at bridge height (~25–30 m) would exceed the 10-metre value due to the vertical wind shear profile. The gust values presented in this report understate the actual wind loading on the vessel's superstructure.
  • This reconstruction quantifies the environmental conditions surrounding the incident. Vessel-specific margin analysis — such as anchor holding capacity versus environmental loading — was outside scope. The environmental reconstruction is complete; the vessel-response modelling was not attempted for this case.
  • ERA5 significant wave height represents open-ocean conditions. In a coastal anchorage, shoaling, refraction, and harbour geometry can modify wave characteristics. Depending on bathymetry and exposure, these effects may increase or decrease wave loading at the vessel's position relative to the open-ocean value. The ERA5 wave height of 4.1 m aligns closely with the 4–5 m range described in the narrative account, suggesting the open-ocean value is representative for this location.
  • ERA5 provides data at standard hourly intervals. The anchor drag began at 04:25 UTC, between the 04:00 and 05:00 extraction points. Both extraction points classify as EXTREME risk (100%). The environmental state at 04:25 falls within the range bounded by these two verified data points.

Methodology

FishIntel Global reconstructs environmental conditions using ECMWF ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis — the same dataset relied upon by meteorological agencies, climate researchers, and aviation safety authorities worldwide.

ERA5 provides hourly estimates on a global grid at approximately 31 km horizontal resolution. All values pass through a unit-gated validation layer enforcing explicit conversion factors and plausibility bounds using Decimal arithmetic. The pipeline is deterministic: identical inputs always produce identical outputs.

This reconstruction operates within defined boundaries. ERA5 does not capture localised effects smaller than its grid resolution. Where a case involves mesoscale phenomena, the reconstruction presents ERA5 as the synoptic baseline, not the peak event conditions.

Closing Statement

Maritime incidents are frequently described after the fact using qualitative or semi-quantitative environmental descriptions: "strong winds," "heavy swell," "poor visibility." These descriptions are valuable for narrative understanding but do not quantify the decision-time environment with the precision required by insurers, regulators, and legal professionals.

Environmental reconstruction bridges this gap. By reconstructing the conditions that existed at the time a critical decision was made — using data sources that were available or forecastable at that time — it becomes possible to evaluate what was objectively knowable before the outcome occurred.

This does not mean that every incident was preventable. It means that the environmental context of every maritime decision can now be quantified, compared against published forecasts, and assessed for foreseeability. The data exists. The question is whether it was used.
