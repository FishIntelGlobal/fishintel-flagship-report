Executive Summary

Incident type: Grounding following anchor drag. A foreign-registered bulk carrier, anchored in ballast at a UK coastal anchorage, dragged its anchor during a severe winter storm and grounded on a public beach. Successfully refloated on the next high water. No injuries, no pollution.

Core question: What environmental conditions were objectively knowable at the time the decision was made to remain at anchor on a lee shore overnight, and at what point did those conditions make the vessel's position untenable?

Method: ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data was extracted at the anchorage coordinates across a three-point time window (03:00–05:25 UTC) and processed through the FishIntel deterministic reconstruction pipeline. The reconstruction quantifies sustained wind, gust speed, significant wave height, atmospheric pressure, and visibility at each extraction point.

Key result: The vessel was in a HIGH-risk environmental regime before the anchor began to drag. ERA5 reconstruction shows sustained 10 m winds of between 25.3 and 27.0 knots (Beaufort force 6–7), gusts of 41.6–44.8 knots, and significant wave height of 3.8–4.1 metres across the incident window. The narrative description of "Force 9–10" appears to reflect the combined severity of locally generated wind waves and remotely generated swell propagating into the anchorage from the open English Channel — not sustained local wind at the 10 m reference height. The dominant forcing mechanisms for anchor drag were heavy swell loading on a vessel in ballast combined with severe gust intensity, rather than sustained storm-force local wind alone.

Analytical finding: Environmental reconstruction separates local wind forcing from remotely generated swell, revealing that the conventional Beaufort estimate in the narrative account conflated two distinct physical mechanisms. The hazardous anchoring regime was already established by 03:00 UTC — more than 90 minutes before the anchor began to drag.

The vessel had sailed from a European port following a lengthy port state control detention. Unable to take bunkers before departure due to unpaid debts, she was ordered to proceed to North Africa for refuelling but had insufficient fuel for the passage. After several days alternating between anchoring and loitering at slow speed off the south-west coast of England, the vessel was authorised to take bunkers at a local port.

She anchored in the bay approximately 1 nautical mile off a public beach, in a charted depth of 17 metres, using the port anchor with 7 shackles of cable (scope ratio approximately 9:1 at high water). A delivery of marine diesel was completed, but a subsequent lube oil delivery was not fulfilled. The vessel was unable to embark on her intended passage.

On the evening before the grounding, the master was aware that the weather forecast predicted southerly winds up to Beaufort force 8. He wrote in his night orders that the main engine should be at immediate readiness for wind speeds exceeding 16–17 m/s (31–33 knots). He discussed the risk of the vessel dragging anchor with the bridge watchkeepers.

By 0400 the following morning, the wind was gusting over 52 knots with a 4–5 metre swell. At 0425, the anchor began to drag. The master was called, began heaving the anchor, and attempted to proceed to sea. The anchor appeared to be fouled by a substantial length of chain, which distracted the crew and cost critical time. The vessel fell beam-to the wind, making over 2 knots leeway towards the shore. Despite full engine power and helm, the master was unable to turn the vessel into the wind. She grounded at 0525.

Local harbour resources coordinated the refloating operation. With the weather moderating, the vessel was pulled off the beach by harbour tugs at 1415 the same day, assisted by her own main engine. An underwater survey subsequently revealed hull plate deformation, breached tanks, and partial bilge keel detachment. There were no injuries and no pollution.

Analytical Note — Sustained Wind vs Sea-State Severity

The narrative account describes conditions as "Beaufort force 9 to 10" (47–55 knots sustained). ERA5 reconstruction shows 10 m sustained wind of 27.0 knots (Beaufort force 6–7) at the anchorage position. These are not equivalent measurements and the difference is analytically significant.

ERA5 reports the 10-metre neutral-stability wind speed averaged over a ~31 km grid cell. The MAIB Beaufort classification is derived from sea-state observation at the vessel's position — a method that conflates locally generated wind waves with remotely generated swell. In this case, a 4+ metre swell was propagating into the anchorage from the open English Channel, generated by stronger winds over a long fetch offshore. The sea state at the anchorage therefore appeared consistent with Force 9–10 conditions, even though the local sustained wind at 10 m was materially lower.

This distinction matters operationally. The dominant forcing mechanisms for anchor drag in this case were heavy swell loading on a vessel in ballast (high windage area, large heaving motion in 4 m swell) combined with severe gust loading (45 kt at 10 m, likely exceeding 50 kt at bridge height). Sustained local Force 9–10 wind was not required to produce the observed outcome.

Methodology

FishIntel Global reconstructs the environmental conditions surrounding maritime incidents using ECMWF ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data — the same dataset relied upon by meteorological agencies, climate researchers, and aviation safety authorities worldwide.

ERA5 provides hourly estimates of atmospheric, land-surface, and ocean-wave variables on a global grid at approximately 31 km horizontal resolution. It assimilates millions of observations per cycle and is continuously quality-controlled by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts. ERA5 data is freely available and independently verifiable by any party.

The FishIntel reconstruction pipeline extracts environmental variables at the precise coordinates and time window of each incident. All values pass through a unit-gated validation layer that enforces explicit conversion factors and plausibility bounds using Decimal arithmetic — preventing the silent unit errors that are a known source of analytical failure in environmental data processing. The pipeline is deterministic: identical inputs always produce identical outputs.

This reconstruction operates within defined boundaries. ERA5 is a gridded reanalysis product, not a point measurement. It does not capture localised effects smaller than its grid resolution, such as harbour-scale wind funnelling or shallow-water wave refraction. Where a case involves such effects, the reconstruction states this limitation explicitly. The system is designed to produce defensible evidence, not to overstate its precision.

FishIntel's role is complementary to national maritime accident investigation. Investigation bodies establish causes and circumstances. FishIntel quantifies the environmental context in which operational decisions were made, using data sources that existed at the time of those decisions. The focus is on what was knowable, not on who is to blame.

Closing Statement

Maritime incidents are frequently described after the fact using qualitative or semi-quantitative environmental descriptions: "strong winds," "heavy swell," "poor visibility." These descriptions are valuable for narrative understanding but do not quantify the decision-time environment with the precision required by insurers, regulators, and legal professionals.

Environmental reconstruction bridges this gap. By reconstructing the conditions that existed at the time a critical decision was made — using data sources that were available or forecastable at that time — it becomes possible to evaluate what was objectively knowable before the outcome occurred.

This does not mean that every incident was preventable. It means that the environmental context of every maritime decision can now be quantified, compared against published forecasts, and assessed for foreseeability. The data exists. The question is whether it was used.
