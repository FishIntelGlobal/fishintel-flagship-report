Executive Summary

Incident type: Fatal man overboard. A crew member on a UK-registered creel fishing vessel (18.2m) was dragged overboard after becoming entangled in the back rope during manual creel shooting operations approximately 16nm north-west of Cape Wrath, Scotland.

Core question: What environmental conditions were objectively present during the hours preceding the incident, and did those conditions determine the operational mode that created the hazard exposure?

Method: ERA5 reanalysis data was extracted at the incident coordinates across a five-point time window (14:00–18:00 UTC) and processed through the FishIntel deterministic reconstruction pipeline with FIG_UNIT_GATE validated conversion.

Key result: ERA5 reconstructed sustained winds of 24.4–25.7 kt (Force 6) with gusts of 34.5–36.3 kt across the full reconstruction window. All five timestamps fall within or above the Force 6 operating band (22–27 kt) above which, according to the MAIB report, the vessel’s self-shooting system could not be used. The reconstruction runner classifies all timestamps as HIGH risk.

Analytical finding: The MAIB-stated “35 knots” likely reflects peak gust speed rather than sustained wind. ERA5 gust at incident time (36.1 kt) is consistent with the MAIB figure. The sustained Force 6 conditions were present for the entire reconstruction window, were synoptic-scale, and would typically be represented in marine weather forecasts.

On 5 February 2018, the creel fishing vessel was operating approximately 16nm north-west of Cape Wrath, Scotland. At approximately 1815–1820 UTC, a crew member was dragged overboard after his left leg became entangled in the back rope while the crew were manually shooting a fleet of creels. He was recovered approximately 10 minutes later but could not be revived.

The vessel was on a north-easterly course at 5 knots SOG. The self-shooting system was not in use because, according to the MAIB report, conditions exceeded its Force 6 operating limit (22–27 kt). Manual shooting placed the crew in direct proximity to the running back rope without physical barriers.

Methodology

FishIntel Global reconstructs historical environmental conditions using ERA5 reanalysis data — the fifth-generation atmospheric reanalysis produced by ECMWF. ERA5 provides hourly estimates of atmospheric and ocean-wave variables on a 0.25° (~31 km) horizontal grid from 1940 to the present.

The reconstruction pipeline extracts ERA5 values at the nearest grid point to the incident location for each timestamp in the analysis window. All environmental values pass through the FIG Unit Gate — a deterministic conversion module using exact rational arithmetic (Decimal-based) to transform ERA5 source-native units (m/s, Pascals, Kelvin) into maritime comparison units (knots, hPa, °C). No floating-point arithmetic is used in the evidence conversion chain.

Raw ERA5 data files are cryptographically sealed at the point of download (SHA-256) before any processing occurs, establishing a tamper-evident provenance chain from the data source through to the final reconstruction values.

Coordinate note: The incident position coordinates (58.814°N, 5.250°W) are working values derived from the MAIB description. ERA5 grid snap: 58.75°N, 5.25°W. A coordinate sensitivity check confirmed both candidate positions produce materially equivalent findings (wind delta ≤3.0 kt, identical wave field, both above Force 6 at all timestamps).

Environmental Operational Findings

Finding 1 — Sustained Wind Within Force 6 Operational Band

ERA5 reconstructed sustained wind speeds ranged from 24.4 to 25.7 knots (10-metre sustained) across the entire 4-hour pre-incident window. The vessel’s self-shooting system operating limit was Force 6 (22–27 knots, MAIB Report p.17). All five reconstruction timestamps fall within or above this operational band. According to the MAIB report, these conditions meant the self-shooting system could not be used, and manual creel shooting was employed, placing crew in direct proximity to the running back rope without physical barriers.

Finding 2 — MAIB-Stated 35 Knots Likely Reflects Peak Gust

The MAIB report states “south-west 35kts” (p.8, p.12). ERA5 reconstructed sustained 10-metre wind at the incident time (1800 UTC) was 25.7 kt. ERA5 reconstructed gust speed at 1800 UTC was 36.1 kt. This suggests the MAIB’s 35 kt value likely reflects peak gust speed rather than mean sustained wind. Both sustained and gust values independently confirm conditions exceeded Force 6. The distinction is analytically relevant: sustained Force 6, not Force 8, was sufficient to be within the operational band above which manual shooting was employed.

Finding 3 — Conditions Were Sustained and Building

Sustained wind was stable between 24.4 and 25.7 kt across the full 4-hour window, with no sudden increase or decrease. Wave height built progressively from 3.25 m at 1400 UTC to 3.68 m at 1800 UTC. Pressure fell steadily from 1019.7 to 1017.2 hPa. These conditions were synoptic-scale, not transient. They would have been identifiable from standard marine weather forecasts hours before the 1400 UTC start of this reconstruction window.

Finding 4 — Elevated Gust Factor Throughout Exposure Window

The ratio of gust speed to sustained wind ranged from 1.35 to 1.46 across the window, with gusts reaching 34.5–36.3 kt (Force 8). These gusts would have caused significant vessel motion during manual operations on an exposed working deck, increasing operational difficulty and reducing the ability to maintain safe footing during manual deck work.

Finding 5 — Wave Conditions Were Building

Significant wave height increased from 3.25 m at 1400 UTC to 3.68 m at 1800 UTC — a 13% increase over 4 hours. The building sea state compounded the operational hazard: vessel motion increased progressively, making manual deck operations more dangerous with each passing hour. The decision to continue manual shooting at 1800 UTC was made in worse conditions than when shooting began earlier.

Industry Safety Findings

The following findings are derived from the environmental reconstruction evidence and are relevant to fishing vessel operators, maritime insurers, and safety regulators.

ISF-1: Environmental Foreseeability

The reconstructed conditions were synoptic-scale (resolved by ERA5 at 31 km horizontal resolution), sustained over at least 4 hours, and showed a consistent SSW wind pattern with slowly building seas and falling pressure. These conditions were not sudden, localised, or unpredictable. They were synoptic-scale and would typically be represented in marine weather forecasts available to vessel operators.

ISF-2: Operational Threshold Breach Creates Continuous Hazard Exposure

ERA5 confirms that sustained wind was within or above the Force 6 self-shooting threshold band at every timestamp in the reconstruction window. Once conditions cross an equipment-specific operational limit, the resulting manual operational mode creates a continuous hazard exposure that persists for the entire duration of those conditions. Equipment-specific operational limits should be treated as hard safety boundaries, not advisory thresholds.

ISF-3: Duration of Exposure Multiplies Risk

The conditions associated with manual operations were sustained for at least 4 hours (the reconstruction window). Manual creel shooting at a rate of one creel every 10 seconds (MAIB Report p.17) means approximately 360 creel deployments per hour. Each deployment cycle exposes crew to the running back rope. Over 4 hours of manual operations, the cumulative number of exposure cycles is substantial, making an entanglement event increasingly probable over time.

ISF-4: MAIB Wind Values May Represent Gust Rather Than Sustained Wind

The ERA5 reconstruction shows sustained wind of 25.7 kt at incident time with gusts of 36.1 kt. The MAIB-stated “35 knots” aligns more closely with the gust value than the sustained value. Insurers and regulators referencing MAIB wind figures should be aware that stated values may represent peak conditions rather than mean sustained wind. For threshold-based operational decisions (such as self-shooting limits), the sustained wind is the relevant comparator — and in this case, sustained Force 6 was sufficient to place the vessel within the operational band associated with manual shooting.

ISF-5: Weather as Operational Mode Determinant

While the proximate cause of this fatality was entanglement in running rope, the environmental conditions were the necessary precondition that, according to the MAIB report, led to the hazardous operational mode. Without sustained winds within or above Force 6, the self-shooting system may have been operable and crew may not have been positioned adjacent to the running back rope without physical barriers. Environmental risk assessment for fishing operations should consider not only direct threats to vessel safety but the operational mode consequences of weather conditions.

Analytical Scope Note

Scope Limitation

This case involves a crew-level hazard (man overboard due to entanglement during fishing operations). The FishIntel T_irr (Time-to-Irreversibility) framework is applied to vessel-level hazards — capsize, flooding, grounding, and loss of vessel control. T_irr analysis is not applied to this case.

The environmental reconstruction establishes: (1) what conditions were objectively present, (2) whether those conditions were foreseeable, (3) how those conditions related to the operational mode described in the MAIB report, and (4) the duration of the exposure window.

Closing Statement

Environmental reconstruction cannot prevent maritime accidents. What it can do is establish, with deterministic precision, what conditions were objectively present at the time of an incident and whether those conditions were foreseeable from the data and forecasts available to decision-makers.

In this case, the environmental conditions were sustained, synoptic-scale, and building over a period of hours. They were of a type and intensity that would typically appear in standard marine weather forecasts. According to the MAIB report, these conditions placed the vessel’s operational mode within or above the Force 6 self-shooting system limit, leading to manual operations that exposed crew to running gear without physical barriers.

The question this reconstruction leaves for investigators, insurers, and regulators is whether the conditions that created the operational exposure were objectively knowable before the decision to commence manual operations was made.
